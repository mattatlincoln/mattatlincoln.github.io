Measures of central tendency¶

Where is the middle of the distribution?

We have looked at the mean, which we saw is also termed the expectation value for a random variable, of a random variable.

There are two other common measures of the centre of the distribution


$\textbf{Mean}$ of data or expectation value of a random variable

$\textbf{Mode}$, which is the most commonly occurring value in the distribution

$\textbf{Median}$, which is middle value of the distribution if values are listed in order, for an odd number of values. Or the mean of the two central values, for even numbers of data points.

These measures are often applied to samples, which may or may not come from some idealised probability distribution.

Median for discrete data¶

For a discrete set of data the median is easily defined:

  • median, which is middle value of the distribution if values are listed in order, for an odd number of values. Or the mean of the two central values, for even numbers of data points.
$$ (1,2,3,4,5) $$

has median 3, the central value.

$$ (1,2,3,4,5,6) $$

has median 3.5 - the mean of 3 and 4.

Algorithmically, we sort the list of numbers, check whether there are an odd or even number, then select the central value, or the central two and average.

We could also think of this as defining a partition that cuts the data in to two pieces, of equal weight - i.e. with equal numbers of data in each partition.

Median for a discrete random variable¶

We could also think of this as defining a partition that cuts the data in to two pieces, of equal weight - i.e. with equal numbers of data in each partition.

The median is the value where it is likely, i.e. $P /geq 0.5$, that the variable would take a value greater than or equal, or less than or equal to.

Remember that the cumulative distribution function of a discrete random variable $Z$ is given by

$$ F_Z(a) = P(Z \leq a) = \sum_{z \leq a} p_Z(z) $$

If we look back at the list $(1,2,3,4,5)$ and convert it to a random variable - say that each value is equally likely, we get

$$ p_Z(z) = 1/5, z = 1,2,3,4,5 $$

now

$$ F_Z(z) = \begin{cases} \hfill 0, & z \lt 1 \\ \hfill \frac{1}{5}, & 1 \leq z \lt 2 \\ \hfill \frac{2}{5}, & 2 \leq z \lt 3 \\ \hfill \frac{3}{5}, & 3 \leq z \lt 4 \\ \hfill \frac{4}{5}, & 4 \leq z \lt 5 \\ \hfill 1, & z \geq 5 \\ \end{cases} $$

We can deduce that the median must be 3:

$P(Z \leq 3) = F(3) = \frac{3}{5}$.

The other equality is a bit more tricky

$$ P(Z \geq 3) = \sum_{z \geq 3} p_Z(z) = p_Z(3) + p_Z(4) + p_Z(5) = \frac{3}{5} $$

Median for continuous data¶

The computation of the Median for a continuous random variable is more interesting.

Recall cumulative distribution function of a random variable gives the probability that the random variable will take on a value equal to, or smaller than, the argument of the function.

For a continuous random variable $X$

$$ F_X(a) = \int_{-\infty}^{a} f(x) dx = P(X \leq a) $$

If we take a value $a$ such that $F_X(a)$ this means that at that value the probability

$$ P(X \leq a) = P(X \gt a) = \frac{1}{2} $$

If we think about it this would also apply to the discrete case. If we take a random value from our list, half the time it will be smaller than our median, and half the time it will be larger.

From this argument, we can say that $a$ is the median of the random variable $X$.

Summary of calculating the Median¶



  • median of a discrete set of data is the middle value of the distribution if values are listed in order, for an odd number of values. Or the mean of the two central values, for even numbers of data points.

  • median of a discrete random variable $Z$ is the value $z$ such that $P(Z \leq z) \geq 0.5$ and $P(Z \geq z) \geq 0.5$.

  • median of a continuous vandom variable $X$ is the value $x$ such that $F(x) = \frac{1}{2}$.

    Quartiles¶

    If we take $n = 4$, then we would make 3 cuts in the data - we've quartered it.

    the values at which we make the three cuts are called quartiles:

    • first quartile, $Q_1$, is defined by $F(Q_1) = \frac{1}{4}$
    • second quartile, $Q_2$, is defined by $F(Q_2) = \frac{2}{4}$
    • third quartile, $Q_3$, is defined by $F(Q_3) = \frac{3}{4}$

    Note again that $Q_2$ is the median.

    example

    consider a probability density function for the random variable $Y$

    $$ f_Y(y) = \Bigg \{ \begin{array}{ll} 2e^{-2y} & \mbox{y $\gt$ 0 },\\ 0 & \mbox{ otherwise} \end{array} $$

    The cumulative distribution function is

    $$ F_Y(a) = \int_0^a f(y) \text{d}y = 1-e^{-2a} $$

    we can then calculate any quartile as

    $$ F_Y(Q_n) = \frac{n}{4} $$

    This implies, making $F_Y(Q_n)$ explicit, that

    $$ 1 - e^{-2 Q_n} = \frac{n}{4} $$

    and rearranging we get

    $$ Q_n = -\frac{\ln(1-\frac{n}{4})}{2} $$

    So we have

    $$ \begin{align} Q_1 = -\frac{\ln(1-\frac{1}{4})}{2} \approx 0.1438 \\ Q_2 = -\frac{\ln(1-\frac{2}{4})}{2} \approx 0.3466 \\ Q_3 = -\frac{\ln(1-\frac{3}{4})}{2} \approx 0.6931 \end{align} $$

    Markovs's Inequality¶

    Lets actually use all this machinery that we've built up to see if we can say anything general about a distribution.

    Let $X$ be a continuous random variable with probability density function $f(x)$.

    We can show that if $X$ only takes on positive values that

    $$ P(X\geq a) \leq \frac{\text{E}[X]}{a} $$

    Now

    $$ E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} xf(x)\text{d}x \\ = \int_{-\infty}^{a} xf(x)\text{d}x + \int_{a}^{\infty} xf(x)\text{d}x $$

    Now we use the condition that $X$ is non-negative to set up an inequality

    $$ \begin{align} \text{E}[X] & = \int_{-\infty}^{a} xf(x)\text{d}x + \int_{a}^{\infty} xf(x)\text{d}x \\ & \geq \int_{a}^{\infty} xf(x)\text{d}x \\ & \geq \int_{a}^{\infty} af(x)\text{d}x \\ & = a \int_{a}^{\infty} f(x)\text{d}x \\ & = aP(X\geq a) \end{align} $$

    Which proves our initial statement.

    Chebyshev's Inequality¶

    We've proved that

    $$ P(X\geq a) \leq \frac{\text{E}[X]}{a} $$

    Take the particular case of $a = k^2$. And consider the expectation value of $g(X)=(X-\text{E[X]})^2$ we get

    $$ P \big((X-\text{E}[X])^2\geq k^2\big) \leq \frac{\text{E}[(X-\text{E}[X])^2]}{k^2} = \frac{\sigma_X^2}{k^2} $$

    As long as the mean of $X$, $\mu$, and its variance, $\sigma^2$, are finite then we can equivalently say

    $$ P \Big(\big| X-\text{E}[X] \big| \geq k\Big) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2} $$

    or if we define a new $k'$ in units of the standard deviation, $\sigma_X$, $k = k'\sigma_X)$, we get

    $$ P \Big(\big|X-\text{E}[X]\big| \geq k'\sigma_X \Big) \leq \frac{1}{k'^2} $$

    this result is known as Chebyshev's Inequality.

    Implication of Chebyshev's Inequality¶

    What does

    $$ P \Big(\big|X-\text{E}[X]\big| \geq k'\sigma_X \Big) \leq \frac{1}{k'^2} $$

    tell us?

    Lets take k' = 2 to be concrete. Then

    $$ P \Big(\big|X-\text{E}[X]\big| \geq 2\sigma_X \Big) \leq \frac{1}{4} $$

    or put another way, 75% of the probability distribution is within 2 standard deviations of the mean.

    Only at most 11.1% of the data will be further than 3 standard deviations from the mean.

    We have made no assumptions at all about the distribution - it applies to any random variable!

    It is generally a rather loose bound, if we know something about the real distribution we can do (much) better

    example

    suppose it is known that the number of items produced in a factory during a week is a random variable, $X$, with mean 50.

    • what can be said about the probability that this week's production exceeds 75?
    • If the variance of the production is known to equal 25, then can be said about ithe probability that the production this week will be between 40 and 60 items?
    • we can use Markov's inequality $$ P(X\geq a) \leq \frac{\text{E}[X]}{a} $$ here we know that ${\text{E}[X]}$ is 50, and the production is a random variable that does not take on negative values. Plugging in the numbers we have $$ P(X \geq 75) \leq \frac{50}{75} = \frac{2}{3} $$ So not very useful in this case.
    • By Chebyshevs inequality in the form $$ P \big(| X-\text{E}[X] | \geq k\big) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2} $$ we can plug in the numbers to get $$ P \big(|X-50| \geq 10 \big) \leq \frac{25}{10^2} = \frac{1}{4} $$ so the probability that the production is between 40 and 60 is at least $1 - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4}$.

    Equivalent probabilities¶

    In the last expression we manipulated equivalent probabilities

    $$ \begin{align} P \big(\{|X-50| \geq 10 \}\big) & = P(\{X - 50 > 10 \cup X - 50 < -10\}) \\ & = P(\{X > 60 \cup X < 40\}) \\ & = P(\{40 < X < 60 \}^C) \\ & = 1 - P(40 < X < 60 ) \end{align} $$

    If we remember the things in the probabilities are really sets, we can rearrange them to get them into a useful form, and get different statements that define the same sets and hence the same probabilities.

    Weak law of large numbers¶

    We're getting a bit ahead of ourselves, but let us go ahead and follow through a remarkable implication of the inequality we've just derived.

    Let $X_1,X_2, \ldots,X_n$ be a sequence of independent and identically distributed random variables, each having $\text{E}[X] = \mu$. Then for any $\epsilon \gt 0$, $$ P \Bigg(\Bigg|\frac{X_1+X_2+ \cdots+X_n}{n} - \mu\Bigg| \gt \epsilon \Bigg) \to 0 \text{ as $n \to \infty$} $$

    example

    If the $X_i$ correspond to consequtive tosses of a coin, and we give them a value 0 if they come up a tail and 1 a head then the weak law of large numbers will say that

    $$ P \Bigg(\Bigg|\frac{X_1+X_2+ \cdots+X_n}{n} - \frac{1}{2}\Bigg| \gt \epsilon \Bigg) \to 0 \text{ as $n \to \infty$} $$

    In this case, as $\text{E}[X_i]=P(\text{\{heads\}})$ we see that the average proportion of the first $n$ flips that differ from the idealised probability $P(E)$ by more than $\epsilon$ tends to 0 as $n$ tends to $\infty$.

    Proof of the weak law of large numbers¶

    If we assume that the variable also have a common variance $\sigma^2$ we can use our results from last week to show that

    $$ \text{E}\Bigg[\frac{X_1+X_2+ \cdots+X_n}{n}\Bigg] = \mu $$

    and

    $$ \text{var}\Bigg(\frac{X_1+X_2+ \cdots+X_n}{n}\Bigg) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} $$

    we just plug these into Chebyshev's equality to get

    $$ P \Bigg(\Bigg| \frac{X_1+X_2+ \cdots+X_n}{n} - \mu\Bigg| \geq \epsilon\Bigg) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{n\epsilon^2} $$

    and the result is proved.
