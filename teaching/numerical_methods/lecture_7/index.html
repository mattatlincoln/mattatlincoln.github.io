    differential equations

    Some differential equations can be solved numerically as follows (more details next term).

    We can approximate a function $y = f(x)$ on a grid of $n$ points that are spaced by $\Delta x$.

    We can approximate derivatives of $y$ by finite differences $$ \frac{\text{d}^2 y}{\text{d} x^2} \Big|_i = \frac{(y_{i+1}-y_{i})/\Delta x - (y_{i}-y_{i-1})/\Delta x}{\Delta x} = \frac{y_{i+1} - 2y_i + y_{i-1}}{\Delta x^2} $$

    In matrix form we can write this as $$ \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & -2 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & -2 \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} y_0\\ y_1\\ \vdots\\ y_i\\ \vdots\\ y_{n-2}\\ y_{n-1}\\ \end{pmatrix} $$

    We can write $$ \frac{\text{d}^2 y}{\text{d} x^2} = g(x) $$ as $$ \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & -2 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & -2 \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} y_0\\ y_1\\ \vdots\\ y_i\\ \vdots\\ y_{n-2}\\ y_{n-1}\\ \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 y_L\\ g_1 \Delta x^2\\ \vdots\\ g_i \Delta x^2\\ \vdots\\ g_{n-2} \Delta x^2\\ -2 y_R\\ \end{pmatrix} $$ where $y_L$ and $y_R$ are the values of $y$ at the left and right boundaries.

    Find a solution for $y$ in the interval [0,1] when:
    - $g(x) = x, y_L = 0.2, y_R=1.5$ with $n=10, 100, 500$

    Fit your data to a polynomial, does this make sense?

    - Solve when $g(x_{n/2}) = 1/\Delta x$, all other $g_i = 0$ and $y_L = 0, y_R=0$

    Power method

    This is a way of finding the eigenvector with the largest eigenvector

  • Start with a guess $\textbf{x}_0$ at the eigenvector (it must not be orthogonal to the true eigenvector).
  • Update the eigenvector $\textbf{x}_i = \frac{\textbf{A} \textbf{x}_{n-1}}{||\textbf{A} \textbf{x}_{n-1}||}$

    • Find the largest eigenvector of $$ \textbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 & -1 & -1\\ -1 & 2 & 0 & 0\\ -1 & 0 & 3 & 1\\ -1 & 0 & 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}.$$ using the power method.

    We basically used this when studying Markov Chains in year 1. What is the stationary state of a system described by a Markov chain with this transition matrix? $$ P=\begin{bmatrix}0.9&0.075&0.025\\0.15&0.8&0.05\\0.25&0.25&0.5\end{bmatrix} $$.

    Power method, why does it work?

    If our matrix is diagonalizable it has a set of orthogonal eigenvectors $\nu_1, \nu_2, \ldots, \nu_n $with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n$ with $|\lambda _{1}|>|\lambda _{j}|$ for $j>1$.

    Any vector can be expanded as a linear combination the $\nu_i$: $$ b_{0}=c_{{1}}v_{{1}}+c_{{2}}v_{{2}}+\cdots +c_{{m}}v_{{m}}. $$

    If we apply our matrix $\textbf{A}$ to $b_0$ $k$ times we get $$ {\begin{array}{lcl}A^{{k}}b_{0}&=&c_{{1}}A^{{k}}v_{{1}}+c_{{2}}A^{{k}}v_{{2}}+\cdots +c_{{m}}A^{{k}}v_{{m}}\\&=&c_{{1}}\lambda _{{1}}^{{k}}v_{{1}}+c_{{2}}\lambda _{{2}}^{{k}}v_{{2}}+\cdots +c_{{m}}\lambda _{{m}}^{{k}}v_{{m}}\\&=&c_{{1}}\lambda _{{1}}^{{k}}\left(v_{{1}}+{\frac {c_{{2}}}{c_{{1}}}}\left({\frac {\lambda _{{2}}}{\lambda _{{1}}}}\right)^{{k}}v_{{2}}+\cdots +{\frac {c_{{m}}}{c_{{1}}}}\left({\frac {\lambda _{{m}}}{\lambda _{{1}}}}\right)^{{k}}v_{{m}}\right).\end{array}} $$ using the fact that for the eigenvectors $\textbf{A} \nu_i = \lambda_i \nu_i$.

    Because $\lambda_1$ is larger than any other $\lambda$, all the terms except the first in the bracket tend to zero as $k$ gets larger.

    All Eigenfunctions

    We can find the eigenfunctions of a matrix from the QR algorithm.

    It can be shown that the sequence $$ \begin{align*} \textbf{A}^{0} & = \textbf{A} \\ \textbf{A}^{k+1} & = \textbf{R}^{k} \textbf{Q}^{k} \end{align*} $$ converges to a upper triangular matrix with the eigenvalues of $\textbf{A}$ as its diagonal entries.

    In the sequence $\textbf{Q}^{k}$ and $\textbf{R}^{k}$ are formed by the QR decomposition of $\textbf{A}^{k}$ $$\textbf{A}^{k} = \textbf{Q}^{k} \textbf{R}^{k}$$

    It can also be shown that the sequence of matrix products $$ \textbf{Q}^{0} \textbf{Q}^{1} \textbf{Q}^{2} \cdots \textbf{Q}^{k-1} \textbf{Q}^{k} $$ converges to the a matrix with the eigenvectors of $\textbf{A}$ as the column entries.

    Modify the QR algorithm code to also compute and output the eigenvectors of a matrix.

    Test it on simple systems and compare to using Gauss Elimination.

    Diagonalization

    We can use the eigenfunctions to define matrix functions.

    We can use the matrix of eigenfunctions $\textbf{P}$ to perform a similarity transform on our matrix, taking it to diagonal form $\textbf{D}$. Where $\textbf{D}$ is a diagonal matrix with the eigenvectors of $\textbf{A}$ as its diagonal elements. $$ \textbf{P}^{-1} \textbf{A}\textbf{P} = \textbf{D} $$

    verify this using your QR code for $$ \textbf{A}=\begin{bmatrix}1&3\\2&1\end{bmatrix} $$

    Matrix functions

    We can use the diagonal form to define functions of matrices.

    $$ f(\textbf{A}) = \textbf{P} f(\textbf{D})\textbf{P}^{-1} $$

    Find $\textbf{A}^4$ and $\textbf{A}!$ for $$ \textbf{A}=\begin{bmatrix}1&3\\2&1\end{bmatrix} $$

    Optimization of matrices

    The minimum of the quadratic form $$ f(\textbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2}\textbf{x}^T \textbf{A x} - \textbf{b}^T\textbf{x} $$ is given by the solution of $\textbf{Ax} - \textbf{b}= 0$

    Find the minimum of $f(\textbf{x})$ where $\textbf{x}^T = (-2,-1,0,1,2)$, $A_{ij} = 2 \delta_{ij}$ and $b_i = 1$ where $\delta_{ij}$ is the Kronecker delta.

    Pen and paper - by looking at the result of differentiating $f(\textbf{x})$ by $x_i$ derive the above formula for the minima of $f(\textbf{x})$.
