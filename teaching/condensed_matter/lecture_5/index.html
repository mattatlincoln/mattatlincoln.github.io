    • Bragg condition¶

    In the simplest case we are looking for the conditions of constructive interference between diffracted rays in the crystal.

    The extra path length should be a multiple of the wavelength $2d \sin \theta =n\lambda $

    A crystal (lattice) plane is a plane containing at least 3 non-colinear points of a lattice
    A family of lattice (crystal) planes is an infinite set of equally spaced parallel planes, which all together contain every point of the lattice.


    The families of lattice planes are in one-to-one correspondence with the possible directions of reciprocal vectors, to which they are normal. The lattice spacing between the lattice planes is $$ d = 2 \pi / |\mathbf{G_{min}}| $$

    where $\mathbf{G_{min}}$ is the minimum length reciprocal vector in this normal direction.

    Proof of last statement

    Consider a set of points $\mathbf{r}$ such that for some integer $m$

    $$ \mathbf{G} \cdot \mathbf{r} = 2 \pi m $$

    Which defines an infinite series of planes perpendicular to $\mathbf{G}$.

    Since $e^{i \mathbf{G} \cdot \mathbf{r}} = 1$ we know that every lattice point will be a member of one of these planes, from our definition of $\mathbf{G}$.

    Now the spacing between two of these planes will be given by

    $$ \mathbf{G} \cdot (\mathbf{r}_{m+1} - \mathbf{r}_m) = 2 \pi $$

    so in the direction parallel to $\mathbf{G}$ the spacing, $d$, is given by

    $$ d = \frac{2 \pi}{|\mathbf{G} |} $$

    you can argue that taking the minimum length $\mathbf{G}$ will then still contain all the lattice points and no extra planes that do not cut lattice points. Then

    $$ d = \frac{2 \pi}{|\mathbf{G}_{min} |} $$

    gives the spacing of these lattice planes.

    As a reciprocal lattice vector is an integer multiple of the reciprocal lattice vectors, we can label them by the integers, which then will also index the planes.

    Miller Indices¶

    A family of lattice planes is determined by three integers h, k, and ℓ, the Miller indices. They are written $(hkl)$, and denote the family of planes orthogonal to $h \mathbf {b_{1}} +k\mathbf {b_{2}} + l \mathbf {b_{3}} $, where $ \mathbf {b_{i}} $ are the basis of the reciprocal lattice vectors.

    By convention, negative integers are written with a bar, as in 3 for −3. The integers are usually written in lowest terms, i.e. their greatest common divisor should be 1.

    Miller indices were introduced in 1839 by the British mineralogist William Hallowes Miller. The method was also historically known as the Millerian system, and the indices as Millerian,[2] although this is now rare.

    The Miller indices are defined with respect to any choice of unit cell and not only with respect to primitive basis vectors, as is sometimes stated.

    There are also several related notations:

    the notation {hkl} denotes the set of all planes that are equivalent to (hkl) by the symmetry of the lattice.

    In the context of crystal directions (not planes), the corresponding notations are: [hkl], with square instead of round brackets, denotes a direction in the basis of the direct lattice vectors instead of the reciprocal lattice; and similarly, the notation denotes the set of all directions that are equivalent to [hkl] by symmetry.

    Another way to look at it is that the planes cut the axes at $[a/h, 0, 0], [0, a/k, 0], [0,0,a/l]$

    Fermi's Golden rule¶

    OK, but how will we make a quantitative estimate of scattering factors? Quantum mechanics...

    We can consider a particle (xray, electron, neutron) characterised by its wavevector

    $$ \mathbf {k} _{i} = \frac{2 \pi }{ \lambda} \mathbf{\hat{k}} _{i} $$

    where $\mathbf{\hat{k}} _{i}$ is the unit vector pointing along $\mathbf{k}$.

    The transition rate per unit time of a particle scattering from the state $\mathbf{k}$ to $\mathbf{k'}$ is given by

    $$ \begin{align} \Gamma(\mathbf{k}, \mathbf{k'}) & = \frac{2 \pi}{\hbar} \Big| \int d\mathbf{r} \frac{e^{-i\mathbf{k'.r}}}{\sqrt{L^3}} V(\mathbf{r}) \frac{e^{i\mathbf{k.r}}}{\sqrt{L^3}}\Big| ^2 \delta(E_{\mathbf{k}'}-E_{\mathbf{k}}) \\ & = \frac{2 \pi}{\hbar} \Big| \int d\mathbf{r} \frac{e^{-i\mathbf{(k - k').r}}}{L^3} V(\mathbf{r})\Big| ^2 \delta(E_{\mathbf{k}'}-E_{\mathbf{k}}) \end{align} $$

    where $ \frac{e^{-i\mathbf{k.r}}}{\sqrt{L^3}}$ is the wavefunction of a free particle with wavevector $\mathbf{k}$, and $ \frac{e^{-i\mathbf{k'.r}}}{\sqrt{L^3}}$ of the particle with wavevector $\mathbf{k'}$.

    This looks a bit terrifying, but it isn't as bad as all that, and we won't need to explicitly evaluate these integrals. The equation is called Fermi's Golden rule. It gives a recipe for how quickly transitions occur (on average) beween an intitial and final state.

    Ignore the rest for now and look at the integral (matrix element)

    $$ \frac{1}{L^3}\int d\mathbf{r} e^{-i\mathbf{(k - k').r}}V(\mathbf{r}) $$

    where the integral is over all of the crystal. It looks more or less like our hopping integral we encountered when doing tight binding - it gives the chance of hopping from state $\mathbf{k}$ to $\mathbf{k'}$. (Also note that this is a fourier transform).

    We know that we can write any location in the crystal as $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{R + x}$ - where $\mathbf{R}$ is a real space lattice vector and $\mathbf{x}$ is a position in the cell.

    Then $$ \begin{align} \frac{1}{L^3} \int d\mathbf{r} e^{-i\mathbf{(k - k').r}} V(\mathbf{r})\\ = \frac{1}{L^3} \sum_{\mathbf{R}} \int_{\text{unit-cell}} d \mathbf{x} e^{-i\mathbf{(k - k').(\mathbf{x + R})}}V(\mathbf{x + R}) \\ \end{align} $$

    and now the trick, because our crystal is periodic we have $V(\mathbf{x + R}) = V(\mathbf{x})$ and we can split our integral into two bits

    $$ \frac{1}{L^3} \sum_{\mathbf{R}} \big[ e^{-i\mathbf{(k - k').\mathbf{R}}} \big] \int_{\text{unit-cell}} d \mathbf{x} e^{-i\mathbf{(k - k').\mathbf{x}}}V(\mathbf{x}) $$

    However, the sum will be zero unless $\mathbf{(k - k')} = \mathbf{G}$, when each term in the sum will be 1. Otherwise the terms in the sum will oscillate and overall sum to zero on average.

    The first sum then just gives us some constant, with the condition that the change in wavevector is a reciprocal lattice vector. This is known as the Laue condition. It is a statement of the conservation of momentum.

    Laue Condition¶

    If $$ \mathbf {G} =h\mathbf {A} +k\mathbf {B} +l\mathbf {C} $$ is the reciprocal lattice vector, we know $$ \mathbf {G} \cdot (\mathbf {a} +\mathbf {b} +\mathbf {c} )=2\pi (h+k+l)$$.

    The Laue equations specify $$ \mathbf {\Delta k} \cdot (\mathbf {a} +\mathbf {b} +\mathbf {c} )=2\pi (h+k+l)$$

    Hence we have $ {\Delta k} =\mathbf {G} $ or $ \mathbf {k} _{o}-\mathbf {k} _{i}=\mathbf {G} $.

    From this we get the diffraction condition:

    \begin{aligned}\mathbf {k} _{o}-\mathbf {k} _{i}&=\mathbf {G} \\(\mathbf {k} _{i}+\mathbf {G} )^{2}&=\mathbf {k} _{o}^{2}\\{k_{i}}^{2}+2\mathbf {k} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {G} +G^{2}&={k_{o}}^{2}\end{aligned}

    \begin{aligned}\mathbf {k} _{o}-\mathbf {k} _{i}&=\mathbf {G} \\(\mathbf {k} _{i}+\mathbf {G} )^{2}&=\mathbf {k} _{o}^{2}\\{k_{i}}^{2}+2\mathbf {k} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {G} +G^{2}&={k_{o}}^{2}\end{aligned}

    Since $(\mathbf {k} _{o})^{2}=(\mathbf {k} _{i})^{2} (\mathbf {k} _{o})^{2}=(\mathbf {k} _{i})^{2}$ (considering elastic scattering) and $\mathbf {G} =-\mathbf {G}$ (a negative reciprocal lattice vector is still a reciprocal lattice vector):

    $$ 2\mathbf {k} _{i} \cdot \mathbf {G} =G^{2}$$ .

    The diffraction condition $ \mathbf {k} _{i} \cdot \mathbf {G} =G^{2}$ can be reduced to the Bragg law $ 2d\sin \theta =n\lambda$ as follows.

    Firstly we can write $\mathbf {k} _{i} = \frac{2 \pi }{ \lambda} \mathbf{\hat{k}} _{i} $, where $\mathbf{\hat{k}} _{i}$ is the unit vector pointing in the direction of the incident ray. Then

    $$ \begin{align*} 2\mathbf {k} _{i} \cdot \mathbf {G} & =G^{2} \\ \frac{4 \pi }{ \lambda } \mathbf{\hat{k}} _{i} \cdot \mathbf {G} & = G^{2} \\ \frac{4 \pi }{ \lambda } G \sin \theta & = G^{2} \\ \implies \frac{2 \pi }{ G } 2 \sin \theta & = \lambda \end{align*} $$ .

    And comparing to Braggs' Law we see that $\frac{2 \pi}{G}$ must be equal to $d$.

    Structure factor¶

    we saw that the intensity of scattering (related to the rate) can be deduced from

    $$ \frac{1}{L^3} \sum_{\mathbf{R}} \big[ e^{-i\mathbf{(k - k') \cdot \mathbf{R}}} \big] \int_{\text{unit-cell}} d \mathbf{x} e^{i\mathbf{G \cdot \mathbf{x}}}V(\mathbf{x}) $$

    the summation reduces to the number of unit cells in the crystal, and is divided by the volume of the crystal, so gives a density.

    The term in the integral is called the structure factor - it is a factor that depends on the structure

    $$ S(\mathbf{G}) = \int_{\text{unit-cell}} d \mathbf{x} e^{i\mathbf{G \cdot \mathbf{x}}}V(\mathbf{x}) $$

    it is often possible to approximate the potential $V(\mathbf{x})$ as the sum of scattering potentials of the atoms in the crystal

    $$ V(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{\text{ atoms } j} V_j(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j) $$

    the form of $\mathbf{V}_j$ will depend on whether we are using X-rays, neutrons, electrons etc to perform the experiment.

    X-rays¶

    x-rays interact with the electron density. We expect the scattering potential to be proportional to the electron density

    $$ V_j (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j) = Z_j g_j (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j) $$

    where $Z_j$ is the number of electrons in the atom. And $g_j$ is some short ranged function. And we get

    $$ S(\mathbf{G}) = \sum_{\text{ atom } j \text{ in the unit cell} } f_j(\mathbf{G}) e^{i\mathbf{G \cdot x}_j} $$

    and $f_j$, called the form factor of atom $j$ is approximately proportional to $Z_j$. This approximation is better at small angles.

    Neutrons¶

    neutrons, being neutral do not interact with electronic density to any appreciable degree, instead the neutron interacts only with the nuclear core, which can be considered as a point particle at the atomic position

    $$ V_j (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j) = f_j \delta (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j) $$

    and $f_j$ is an essentially atomic dependent quantity that determines how strongly atoms of a given type scatter neutrons.

    Example¶

    Caesium Chloride, which has a primitive cubic lattice, and a basis

    $\mathbf{a}_1 = [a,0,0], \mathbf{a}_2 = [0,a,0], \mathbf{a}_3 = [0,0,a]$

    $$ \begin{align} &\text{Cs at } [0.0,0.0,0.0]\\ &\text{Cl at } [0.5a,0.5a,0.5a] \end{align} $$ Now in this case the reciprocal lattices vectors are given by

    $$ \mathbf{G}_{hkl} = h \mathbf{b}_1 + k \mathbf{b}_2 + l \mathbf{b}_3 $$

    and

    $\mathbf{b}_1 = [2 \pi / a,0,0], \mathbf{b}_2 = [0,2 \pi / a,0], \mathbf{b}_3 = [0,0,2 \pi / a]$

    The general form of the structure factor will then be

    $$ \begin{align} S(\mathbf{G})_{hkl} & = \sum_{\text{ atom } j \text{ in the unit cell} } f_j(\mathbf{G}) e^{-i\mathbf{G \cdot x}_j} \\ & = \sum_{\text{ atom } j \text{ in the unit cell} } f_j(\mathbf{G}) e^{2 \pi i (h x_j + k y_j + l z_j)} \end{align} $$

    and for our particular case we get $$ \begin{align} S(\mathbf{G})_{hkl} & = \sum_{\text{ atom } j \text{ in the unit cell} } f_j(\mathbf{G}) e^{-i\mathbf{G \cdot x}_j} \\ & = \sum_{\text{ atom } j \text{ in the unit cell} } f_j(\mathbf{G}) e^{2 \pi i (h x_j + k y_j + l z_j)} \\ & = f_{\text{Cs}} + f_{\text{Cl}}(-1)^{h + k + l} \end{align} $$

    And the scattered wave intensity $I_{hkl} \sim |S_{hkl}|^2$

    Systematic absences¶

    Caesium bcc metal.¶

    Caesium metal can crystallise in the BCC structure. If we work with the conventional unit cell, then the scattering will be described in a very similar way to the previous example.

    $\mathbf{a}_1 = [a,0,0], \mathbf{a}_2 = [0,a,0], \mathbf{a}_3 = [0,0,a]$

    $$ \begin{align} &\text{Cs at } [0.0,0.0,0.0]\\ &\text{Cs at } [0.5a,0.5a,0.5a] \end{align} $$ Now in this case the reciprocal lattices vectors are given by

    $$ \mathbf{G}_{hkl} = h \mathbf{b}_1 + k \mathbf{b}_2 + l \mathbf{b}_3 $$

    and

    $\mathbf{b}_1 = [2 \pi / a,0,0], \mathbf{b}_2 = [0,2 \pi / a,0], \mathbf{b}_3 = [0,0,2 \pi / a]$

    and now the structure factor will be given by $$ \begin{align} S(\mathbf{G})_{hkl} & = \sum_{\text{ atom } j \text{ in the unit cell} } f_j(\mathbf{G}) e^{-i\mathbf{G \cdot x}_j} \\ & = \sum_{\text{ atom } j \text{ in the unit cell} } f_j(\mathbf{G}) e^{2 \pi i (h x_j + k y_j + l z_j)} \\ & = f_{\text{Cs}}(1 + (-1)^{h + k + l}) \end{align} $$

    This means that all the odd reflections are missing for a BCC crystal! By describing the BCC as a primitive cubic with a basis, we ignored some of the symmetry - the layer of atoms at the centre of the unit cells leads to destructive interference with some reflections.

    And the scattered wave intensity $I_{hkl} \sim |S_{hkl}|^2$

    Copper fcc metal¶

    $\mathbf{a}_1 = [a,0,0], \mathbf{a}_2 = [0,a,0], \mathbf{a}_3 = [0,0,a]$

    $$ \begin{align} &\text{Cu at } [0.0,0.0,0.0]\\ &\text{Cu at } [0.0,0.5a,0.5a] \\ &\text{Cu at } [0.5a,0.0,0.5a] \\ &\text{Cu at } [0.5a,0.5a,0.0] \\ \end{align} $$ Now in this case the reciprocal lattices vectors are given by

    $$ \mathbf{G}_{hkl} = h \mathbf{b}_1 + k \mathbf{b}_2 + l \mathbf{b}_3 $$

    and

    $\mathbf{b}_1 = [2 \pi / a,0,0], \mathbf{b}_2 = [0,2 \pi / a,0], \mathbf{b}_3 = [0,0,2 \pi / a]$

    $$ \begin{align} S(\mathbf{G})_{hkl} & = \sum_{\text{ atom } j \text{ in the unit cell} } f_j(\mathbf{G}) e^{-i\mathbf{G \cdot x}_j} \\ & = \sum_{\text{ atom } j \text{ in the unit cell} } f_j(\mathbf{G}) e^{2 \pi i (h x_j + k y_j + l z_j)} \\ & = f_{\text{Cu}}\Big(1 + e^{i \pi(h + k)} + e^{i \pi(k + l)} + e^{i \pi(l + h)}\Big) \end{align} $$

    and this is non zero only if $h,k,l$ are all odd or all even.

    Stucture factor factorizes¶

    Consider ZnS, Zinc Sulfide = FCC with a basis.

    We will use a conventional cubic cell for the FCC, and the ZnS, which is essentially a diamond structure has a basis

    $$ \begin{align} \text{Zn at postiions: } &[0.00,0.00,0.00], [0.50,0.50,0.00], [0.50,0.00,0.50], [0.00, 0.50, 0.50]\\ \text{S at postiions: } &[0.25,0.25,0.25], [0.75,0.75,0.25], [0.75,0.25,0.75], [0.25, 0.75, 0.75] \end{align} $$ in units of $a$.

    The structure factor is given by

    $$ \begin{align} S(\mathbf{G})_{hkl} & = f_{\text{Zn}}\Big(1 + e^{2i \pi(hkl)\cdot(0.5,0.5,0.0)} + e^{2i \pi(hkl)\cdot(0.5,0.0,0.5)} + e^{2i \pi(hkl)\cdot(0.0,0.5,0.5)}\Big) \\ & + f_{\text{S}}\Big(e^{2i \pi(hkl)\cdot(0.25,0.25,0.25)} + e^{2i \pi(hkl)\cdot(0.75,0.75,0.25)} + e^{2i \pi(hkl)\cdot(0.75,0.25,0.75)} + e^{2i \pi(hkl)\cdot(0.25,0.75,0.75)}\Big) \end{align} $$

    but this can be factored into a lattice term, and a basis term

    $$ \begin{align} S(\mathbf{G})_{hkl} & = \Big(1 + e^{i \pi(h + k)} + e^{i \pi(k + l)} + e^{i \pi(l + h)}\Big) \\ & \times \Big(f_{\text{Zn}} + f_{\text{S}}\Big(e^{2i \pi(hkl)\cdot(0.25,0.25,0.25)}\Big) \end{align} $$

    and the first term in the brackets is the same as for an ideal FCC crystal. The second term gives a structure associated specifically with ZnS.

    This follows from the fact that atomic positions are defined by $\mathbf{r_i} = \mathbf{R + x_i}$. The structure factor will always factorize into

    $$ S(\mathbf{G})_{hkl} = S(\mathbf{G})_{hkl}^{\text{lattice}} \times S(\mathbf{G})_{hkl}^{\text{basis}} $$

    We will see that this factorization is very general in the solid state. In its most general form, it is known as Bloch's Theorem.
